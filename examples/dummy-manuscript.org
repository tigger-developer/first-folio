The corridor outside the archive was quiet enough for a whisper to travel. "Are you sure this is the right drawer?" Mira asked. "No," said Rowan, "but it is the only one that hummed when I touched it." The ledger flashed *WAIT* -- then the brass latch answered --- and Mira whispered ~nine-bell~ into her phone.

Mira checked the [[https://example.invalid/archive][archive index]] and found a note where none had been before.[fn:ledger]

- The drawer hummed when touched.
- The latch answered only after the ninth word.
- The map showed a room that had been demolished.

1. Copy the date.
2. Close the ledger.
3. Leave before the bell repeats.

| Name | Value |
|------+-------|
| Bell | Nine |
| Gate | Orchard |

Praesent at lacus vel risus iaculis consequat. Sed porta magna a orci luctus, at venenatis lacus tempor. Morbi posuere, nibh at blandit porttitor, neque erat volutpat nisl, non finibus risus justo vitae lectus.

A short paragraph followed, because not every thought arrives wearing a long coat.

"Then we wait," Mira said. Rowan shook his head. "Waiting is how the clock wins."

The first ledger was written in a hand so precise that every loop seemed measured. Mira copied the date into her notebook, then stopped because the date had copied itself twice more underneath. Rowan watched the ink settle. "That is either a warning," he said, "or the archive has a terrible sense of humour."

They worked by the green desk lamp while the building cooled around them. A pipe clicked in the wall. Somewhere above, a chair scraped over a floor that had been locked since before either of them was born.

"Read the margin," Mira said.

Rowan bent closer. "It says, do not trust the ninth bell."

"There is no ninth bell."

"Then I suppose we are meant to worry about the first eight."

The next page was softer than paper and colder than cloth. It held a list of names neither of them knew, arranged beside times that had not happened yet. One entry had been underlined hard enough to bruise the sheet.

Mira turned the ledger sideways. "This symbol appears beside every crossing."

"That is not a symbol," Rowan said. "It is a map."

The rain worried at the windows with patient fingers. Below them the square was empty, but every puddle reflected a crowd. Mira watched the reflections move where no bodies moved and decided not to mention it until Rowan saw them too.

"If we leave now," he said, "we can pretend this was a catalogue error."

"You cannot catalogue tomorrow by accident."

"Archivists catalogue everything by accident. It is practically a professional method."

She smiled despite herself, then lost the smile when the lamp flickered. The ledger pages lifted without wind. On the exposed sheet, a line of fresh ink appeared one word at a time.

The sentence was short enough to be cruel.

Do not open the orchard gate.

Rowan read it aloud and the room answered with the smell of cut grass. The smell came first as a memory, then as weather. It filled the archive, wet and bright, until the dust on the shelves darkened and the brass labels shone.

"There is no orchard here," Mira said.

"There was," Rowan answered. "Under the north wing. They built over it after the fire."

The clock above the door struck once. Then it struck once again, softer. By the fifth repetition, Mira had stopped counting aloud. By the eighth, Rowan had put one hand over the ledger as though he could keep the ninth bell inside the page.

The ninth bell did not sound. The silence after it did.

Every drawer in the archive opened at once.

[fn:ledger] The ink was still wet when Mira touched the margin.
